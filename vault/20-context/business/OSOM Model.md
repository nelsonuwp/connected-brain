# OSOM: Organised Services Operating Model

**Source:** [https://osom.guide/](https://osom.guide/)

## 1. Overview and Introduction

The **Organised Services Operating Model (OSOM)** is a Creative Commons template operating model designed to help organizations transition to a **service-based approach**. 

This approach stands in direct opposition to traditional *functional models*, where capabilities are grouped merely by skills, and ownership of delivery is fractured across multiple departments. In OSOM, users are put front and center through a simple, cell-based structure paired with centralized governance.

### Origins & Philosophy
* **Roots:** OSOM grew out of years of experience transforming organizations in the public sector, heavily inspired by the practical application of the UK Government Digital Service's (GDS) Service Standard. It is now used across both public and private sectors.
* **Adaptability:** OSOM is a *template*, not a rigid plug-and-play framework. Few organizations map perfectly to it out-of-the-box. Instead, it serves as a general guide that should be carefully adapted. Organizations must be aware of the trade-offs (both positive and negative) they make when compromising on the model to fit their unique context.
* **Objective:** It enables the creation of long-lived, cross-functional service teams that can design, build, and deliver effectively.

## 2. Core Concepts

The model is built around several foundational concepts:

### The Executive
The central capability providing oversight and governance. The Executive governs the network of services and holds Service Managers accountable through various mechanisms and terms of reference. They also advocate for and monitor the adoption of "guides to better decisions" throughout the organization.

### The Service Network
An ecosystem of services designed to address specific user needs. This network represents the actual value-delivery engine of the organization.

### Services and User Needs
OSOM services directly address the needs of users. For any given service, the users may be external to the organization, internal (employees/other systems), or a mixture of both. 

### Service Lifecycle
Services are not static; they progress through a simple, defined lifecycle that is compatible with the GDS Service Manual—spanning from initial **Discovery** all the way to eventual **Retirement**. 

### Making Better Decisions
OSOM advocates for a collection of roughly 40 universally useful patterns of behavior that should be encouraged wherever possible across the organization to streamline operations.

## 3. People (Roles & Structures)

OSOM redefines organizational charts away from functional silos toward product and service-aligned roles:

* **Executive Team Member:** Leaders who sit on the Executive board, responsible for overarching governance, balancing organizational trade-offs, and ensuring the Service Network is healthy.
* **Service Managers:** The single point of accountability for a service. They build, operate, and own the service (and the team required to run it) across all phases of its lifecycle. 
* **Service Team Members:** The practitioners working within long-lived, cross-functional teams dedicated to a specific service.
* **Community of Practice:** Because practitioners are embedded in cross-functional service teams rather than functional silos, Communities of Practice are established to allow people with similar skills (e.g., designers, engineers) to share knowledge, standards, and support.

---

## 4. Processes

OSOM outlines specific operating processes designed to keep services accountable, resilient, and continuously improving:

* **Establishing a New Service:** The governance and funding process required to identify a new user need, assign a Service Manager, and initiate the service lifecycle.
* **Demand Management:** Mechanisms for understanding, prioritizing, and managing the intake of requests and requirements hitting the Service Network.
* **Service Assessments:** Periodic evaluations (inspired by the GDS Service Standard) where services are reviewed against standard criteria to ensure they are meeting user needs, maintaining technical quality, and progressing healthily through their lifecycle.
* **Correction of Errors (COE):** A blameless, structured process for incident management and post-mortems, ensuring the organization learns from operational failures and bakes those learnings back into the service.

---

## 5. Artefacts

To support the processes and the people, OSOM relies on a standardized set of artifacts for visibility and contract-setting:

* **Service Contract:** A document defining the boundaries, expected outputs, inputs, and SLAs/SLOs of a specific service. It clearly articulates what the service provides to its users or to other services in the network.
* **Service Performance Measures:** The specific, tracked metrics used to determine the health, efficiency, and user-satisfaction levels of a service.
* **Correction of Error Document:** The written output of the COE process, detailing what went wrong during an incident, why it happened (root cause analysis), and the actionable steps the Service Team will take to prevent a recurrence.

---

## 6. Strategic Integration

* **Wardley Mapping:** OSOM often utilizes tools like Wardley Maps to dig into the operational and financial aspects of the services. This helps the Executive team visualize the Service Network, understand the maturity of different components, and make strategic decisions about where to invest, outsource, or build custom solutions.
