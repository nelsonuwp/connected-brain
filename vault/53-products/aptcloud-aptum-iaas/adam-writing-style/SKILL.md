# Writing Style Rules

These rules apply to all written output: documents, presentations, speaking notes, emails, markdown, code comments, explanations, and conversational responses. They reflect strong preferences that have been explicitly stated and corrected multiple times.

## No em dashes

Never use em dashes (the long dash: —) in any written output.

Use alternatives instead:

- Commas for parenthetical asides: "The team, which started in January, shipped the feature" instead of "The team — which started in January — shipped the feature"
- Periods to break long sentences into two shorter ones
- Colons when introducing a list or explanation
- Parentheses for brief clarifications

The en dash (–) for ranges like "2024–2026" or "Q2–Q3" is fine. The problem is only the em dash used as punctuation within sentences.

## No dramatic contrast phrases

Never use the "This isn't X, it's Y" or "This is not X. It's Y." construction. This includes all variations:

- "This isn't speculation, it's happening"
- "This is not a slow shift. Companies are making..."
- "This isn't a product update. This is a pitch for..."
- "This is not catch-up. Here's why."

These feel like marketing copy and headline interruptions. They're a rhetorical device that draws attention to its own cleverness rather than making the point directly.

Instead, just state what the thing is. If you want to address a potential misconception, do it naturally: "The data suggests X" rather than "This isn't speculation, it's happening: the data suggests X."

## No bolded emphasis sentences

Don't use bold to make a standalone sentence into a headline or emphasis point in the middle of prose. For example, don't write:

**This is the key insight.** The data shows that...

Instead, let the sentence carry its own weight through clear writing. Bold is fine for labels, headers, names in lists, and structural formatting. The problem is using bold as a substitute for persuasive writing.

## Write in a continuous, matter-of-fact tone

Prefer straightforward phrasing. Let data and logic do the persuading rather than rhetorical devices. State things directly. If a point is important, the content of the point should make that clear without dramatic framing.

Good: "29 of 54 customers grew their total spend during the disruption period."
Bad: "Here's what's remarkable. 29 of 54 customers actually grew."

Good: "The platform combines infrastructure, portal, and managed services in a way that doesn't exist elsewhere in the market."
Bad: "Nobody else does this. And here's why that matters."
